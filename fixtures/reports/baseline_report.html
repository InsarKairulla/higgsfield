# DNA Replication

DNA replication is the biological process by which a cell makes an identical copy of its DNA prior to cell division. It is semiconservative: each resulting double helix contains one original strand and one newly synthesized strand.

Replication begins at specific sites called **origins of replication**. Helicase unwinds the double helix, producing a replication fork. Single-strand binding proteins stabilize the separated strands.

DNA polymerase synthesizes new DNA only in the 5' to 3' direction. This produces a continuous **leading strand** and a discontinuous **lagging strand**, the latter assembled from Okazaki fragments that are later joined by DNA ligase. Short RNA primers made by primase provide the 3'-OH group that polymerase needs to begin extension.

Proofreading by DNA polymerase and subsequent mismatch repair produce error rates on the order of one mistake per billion base pairs.

# Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is the process by which plants, algae, and certain bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. The overall equation is:

    6 CO2 + 6 H2O + light -> C6H12O6 + 6 O2

In plants, photosynthesis occurs in chloroplasts and has two main stages:

1. **Light-dependent reactions** take place in the thylakoid membranes. Chlorophyll absorbs light, driving the splitting of water, the release of oxygen, and the production of ATP and NADPH.
2. **Light-independent reactions** (the Calvin cycle) take place in the stroma. They use ATP and NADPH from the first stage to fix CO2 into three-carbon sugars, which are ultimately assembled into glucose.

Only the visible portion of the spectrum, primarily red and blue wavelengths, is usable for photosynthesis; green light is mostly reflected, which is why plants appear green.
